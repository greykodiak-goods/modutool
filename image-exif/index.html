What your photos quietly reveal

Every photo taken with a phone or camera carries an invisible passenger: EXIF metadata. It records the capture time, the device model, exposure settings — and, whenever location services were on, the exact GPS coordinates of where you stood. Post the original file to a marketplace listing, a forum, or a chat, and anyone who saves it can read those coordinates with free software. That can point straight at your home address.

Large social networks usually strip EXIF on upload, but marketplaces, blogs, email attachments, and cloud links often pass the original file through unchanged. If you are not sure, check — that is exactly what this tool is for.

How the check and the removal work

  • The check reads the raw bytes of your file locally. For JPG it looks for the EXIF segment and, inside it, the marker of the GPS block. It is a fast presence check, not a full metadata viewer — it tells you whether the data is there, which is what matters before sharing.
  • The removal does not try to surgically cut segments out of the file. Instead the image is decoded and re-saved from a clean canvas, so nothing hidden in the original container — EXIF, GPS, XMP, thumbnails, editor traces — can survive into the copy.
  • JPG stays JPG (saved at 92% quality); other formats are saved as PNG, which is lossless. Resolution is never changed, and rotation stored in the orientation tag is baked in so the clean copy displays the right way up.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is EXIF, and why is the GPS part a problem?

EXIF is metadata your camera or phone writes into every photo file: capture time, device model, camera settings — and, if location was on, the exact GPS coordinates of where the photo was taken. The pixels look harmless, but anyone who downloads the original file can read those coordinates and find your home, workplace, or child's school. That is why stripping EXIF before posting photos to marketplaces, forums, or social media matters.

Does removing EXIF change the image quality?

Slightly, and we want to be honest about it: the clean copy is produced by re-encoding the pixels through a canvas, because that is the reliable way to guarantee no metadata is carried over. JPG files are re-saved as JPG at 92% quality — visually indistinguishable for normal photos, but not bit-identical to the original. PNG output is lossless. The resolution is never changed.

Will my photo come out rotated wrong without the orientation tag?

No. Phone photos often store their rotation only as an EXIF orientation tag. This tool decodes the image with that orientation applied first, so the correct rotation is baked into the pixels of the clean copy. The tag itself is gone, but the photo displays the right way up everywhere — including in software that ignores EXIF.

I checked a screenshot and it says no EXIF found — is that normal?

Yes. Screenshots are generated by your operating system, not a camera, so they normally contain no GPS position or camera EXIF. Some apps write minor software metadata, but nothing location-revealing. Photos taken with the camera app are the ones to worry about — check those before sharing the original file.

Is my photo uploaded anywhere during the check?

No. Both the check and the removal read the file bytes directly on your device using your browser's built-in features. You can open the Network tab of your browser's developer tools and confirm there is no upload request. Closing the tab removes everything.
