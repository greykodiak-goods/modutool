How to Use

  1. Drop the image into the area above, or click to select it.
  2. Choose a Split layout. The preview shows dashed lines exactly where the cuts will be — 3 pieces (vertical thirds) is the Instagram panorama carousel format, 3×3 is for profile grids.
  3. Click Split & Download. Each piece is cropped at full original resolution and saved as filename_1, filename_2, and so on. If your browser asks to allow multiple downloads, click Allow.
  4. Upload in the right order: carousels in order 1 → 2 → 3; a 3×3 profile grid in reverse (9 first, 1 last), because Instagram places the newest post at the top-left.

How the pieces are numbered

Tiles are always numbered left to right, top to bottom: in a 3×3 grid, _1 is the top-left tile and _9 the bottom-right. That numbering is what makes the upload order predictable — swipeable carousels go up in order, profile grids go up in reverse. The guide step above spells out both.

When the width or height does not divide evenly, the tool uses whole-pixel cuts and gives the remainder to the last column and row — 1000px into thirds becomes 333 + 333 + 334. No pixels are dropped, none are stretched.

Full resolution, no resampling

The preview you see is scaled down for the screen, but the cutting happens on the original pixels. A 4000×3000 photo split 1×3 produces three files of roughly 1333×3000 each — nothing is downscaled. JPG input is saved as JPG (92% quality), other formats as lossless PNG. As a side effect of re-saving through a clean canvas, EXIF metadata such as GPS location is not carried into the pieces.

💡 Tip: Instagram displays carousel images best when each piece is close to a supported ratio (1:1, 4:5, or 1.91:1). If your panorama is very wide, consider cropping it first with the Crop Image tool so each third lands near 4:5.

Frequently Asked Questions

In what order do the pieces download, and why does it matter for Instagram?

Pieces are numbered left to right, top to bottom — _1 is the top-left tile. For a carousel (the 3-piece vertical split), add the photos in numeric order 1, 2, 3 so they swipe seamlessly. For a 3×3 profile grid it is the opposite: Instagram shows your newest post at the top-left, so upload the LAST tile first (9, 8, 7 … 1) to make the grid assemble correctly.

My browser only downloaded one file. Where are the rest?

Browsers block pages from saving several files at once unless you allow it. The tool spaces the downloads out and your browser will show a prompt like "This site is attempting to download multiple files" — click Allow, then split again. Every piece downloads as a separate numbered file.

Does splitting reduce image quality?

The crop itself is pixel-exact: each tile is cut from the original at full resolution, with no scaling or resampling. Saving does re-encode the file — JPG tiles are written at 92% quality, which is visually indistinguishable for photos, and PNG tiles are lossless. The combined tiles contain exactly the same pixels as the original image.

What happens when the image doesn't divide evenly?

The remainder pixels go to the last column and last row. For example, a 1000px-wide image split into 3 gives tiles of 333, 333, and 334 pixels. That means tiles can differ by a pixel or two — invisible in practice, and no pixels are ever dropped or stretched.

Is my image uploaded to a server?

No. The cutting happens entirely on your device using your browser's built-in canvas feature. You can verify in the Network tab of your browser's developer tools that no file leaves your machine. Closing the tab removes everything.
