How to use

  1. Pick a mode: a deposit (one lump sum sitting for the term), a savings plan (the same amount deposited every month), or a loan repaid in equal monthly payments.
  2. Fill in the amount, annual rate and term in months. There is no button — every figure updates as you type, so you can play what-if with the rate or term. Thousands separators (e.g. 10,000,000) are recognized.
  3. For deposits, choose simple or monthly compound interest. If interest income is taxed where you live, enter the rate in the optional tax field to see after-tax figures (leave it at 0 to see gross interest only).

The formulas, exactly as implemented

  • Deposit, simple: interest = principal × (rate ÷ 100) × (months ÷ 12). Example: 10,000,000 at 3.5% for 12 months → 350,000.
  • Deposit, monthly compound: interest = principal × ((1 + rate ÷ 1200)months − 1). The same deposit above → about 355,670, slightly more because each month's interest starts earning too.
  • Savings plan (simple interest per installment): interest = monthly deposit × (rate ÷ 1200) × n(n+1) ÷ 2. Each installment earns simple interest for the months remaining until maturity — the standard method Korean banks use for installment savings. Example: 500,000/month at 3.5% for 12 months → total deposited 6,000,000, interest 113,750.
  • Loan (equal total payment): M = P × r × (1+r)n ÷ ((1+r)n − 1), with r = annual rate ÷ 12. When the rate is 0%, the payment is simply P ÷ n. Total interest = M × n − P.

Why savings-plan interest looks "small"

A common surprise: 6,000,000 total paid into a 12-month savings plan at 3.5% earns about 113,750 — nowhere near the 210,000 that 6,000,000 would earn as a day-one deposit. Nothing is wrong. Only your first installment is invested for the full 12 months; the second sits for 11 months, and the final one for just 1 month. Summing those periods gives n(n+1)/2 = 78 "deposit-months" instead of 144, so the effective time your money spends in the account is a bit over half the term. When banks advertise a savings-plan rate, remember it applies to each installment's remaining months, not to your final balance.

How an amortized loan payment behaves

With equal total payments (the most common consumer structure), you pay the same amount every month, but its composition shifts: early payments are mostly interest computed on the still-large balance, and later payments are mostly principal. Two practical consequences:

  • Stretching the term lowers the monthly payment but raises total interest — a 100,000,000 loan at 4% costs about 1,012,451/month over 10 years (total interest ≈ 21.5 million) versus about 605,980/month over 20 years (total interest ≈ 45.4 million).
  • Early repayment saves the most near the start, when the balance — and therefore each month's interest share — is largest.
💡 Tip: to compare a savings plan against a deposit fairly, compare the interest per amount-actually-invested-over-time, not the headline rates. A 5% savings plan can earn less cash than a 3% deposit of the same total, purely because of timing.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?

Simple interest is always charged on the original principal only: interest = principal × rate × time. Compound interest adds each period's interest to the principal, so later periods earn interest on interest: with monthly compounding, balance = principal × (1 + rate/12)^months. Over short terms the difference is small, but it grows quickly with time and rate — that is why the compounding basis (monthly, quarterly, annual) matters when comparing products.

Why is the interest on a monthly savings plan so much less than on a lump-sum deposit at the same rate?

Because each monthly installment only earns interest for the months it is actually in the account. In a 12-month plan, the first deposit earns 12 months of interest but the last one earns only 1 month. Averaged out, your money is invested for roughly half the term, so the total interest is close to half of what the same total amount would earn as a day-one lump sum. This calculator uses the standard Korean bank formula: interest = monthly deposit × (rate ÷ 12) × n(n+1)/2.

What formula is used for the loan payment?

The standard amortization (equal total payment) formula: M = P × r × (1+r)^n ÷ ((1+r)^n − 1), where P is the loan amount, r the monthly rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n the number of months. Every month you pay the same amount M; early payments are mostly interest and later ones mostly principal. If the rate is 0%, the payment is simply P ÷ n.

Is tax deducted from the interest?

In most countries interest income is taxed — in South Korea, for example, banks withhold 15.4% of deposit and savings interest at source. This calculator shows pre-tax interest by default; enter your local rate in the optional tax-rate field to see the after-tax figures. Loan payments are not affected by the tax field.

Why do the bank's numbers differ slightly from this calculator?

Banks typically accrue interest by exact days (a 365- or 366-day year) rather than flat months, apply preferential-rate conditions, round at intermediate steps, and may use a different compounding basis. This calculator uses clean monthly arithmetic, so treat its results as close estimates for comparing options — the binding numbers are on the bank's own schedule or contract.
