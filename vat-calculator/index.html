The two directions of a VAT calculation

Every VAT problem is one of two directions, and mixing them up is the single most common error on invoices and receipts.

  • Adding VAT (net → gross): VAT = net × rate, total = net + VAT. At 10%: a 100,000 net amount carries 10,000 of VAT for a 110,000 total.
  • Extracting VAT (gross → net): net = total ÷ (1 + rate), VAT = total − net. At 10%: a 110,000 total divides by 1.1 to give a 100,000 net and 10,000 of VAT.

The trap in the second direction is taking the rate straight off the gross figure. 10% of 110,000 is 11,000 — but the real VAT inside that total is 10,000, because the 10% was charged on the net amount, not on the total. Whenever a price is quoted "VAT included," divide first.

Where you'll meet this calculation

  • Quoting a price — a freelancer or contractor quotes a net fee and needs the VAT-inclusive figure the client will actually pay.
  • Reading a receipt — a consumer price includes VAT; backing it out shows what the seller keeps and what goes to the tax office.
  • Bookkeeping — purchase and sales ledgers record net and VAT separately, so every gross bank-statement figure has to be split.
  • Comparing offers — one supplier quotes ex-VAT, another quotes inclusive; converting both to the same basis is the only fair comparison.

Rates differ by country — 10% in South Korea, 20% in the UK and France, 19% in Germany, and US states use a sales tax added at checkout instead of a VAT baked into the shelf price. The arithmetic is identical either way; only the rate field changes. Note that many systems also exempt categories such as staple foods, medical care and education, and small businesses under a turnover threshold may charge no VAT or a reduced simplified amount — this calculator assumes the standard rate applies to the full amount.

💡 Mental math tip: at the 10% rate, backing VAT out of a total is just dividing by 11 — the VAT inside any gross figure is total ÷ 11 (110,000 ÷ 11 = 10,000). At 20%, it is total ÷ 6.

Rounding, honestly stated

This calculator rounds each figure to the nearest whole currency unit and keeps the three lines consistent, so net + VAT always equals the total you see. Real invoicing systems sometimes truncate instead of rounding, or round per line item rather than per invoice, so an official document can differ from this result by a unit or two. Treat the output as an estimate for checking and planning, not as a substitute for your accounting software or tax authority's figures.

Frequently asked questions

How do I back the VAT out of a VAT-inclusive total?

Divide the gross total by (1 + rate). At the 10% rate that means net = total ÷ 1.1, and VAT = total − net. For example, a 110,000 gross total breaks down into a 100,000 net amount and 10,000 of VAT. A common mistake is taking 10% of the gross (110,000 × 0.1 = 11,000) — that overstates the tax, because the 10% applies to the net amount, not the total.

How does this calculator round the results?

Each figure is rounded to the nearest whole currency unit (standard half-up rounding), and the three lines are kept consistent so that net + VAT always equals the total exactly. Invoicing software and tax authorities may use different rounding conventions (truncation, per-line vs per-invoice rounding), so totals on an official document can differ by a unit or two.

What VAT rate should I use?

The default is 10%, the standard rate in South Korea (and the consumption-tax rate in Japan). Rates vary widely by country — for example 20% in the UK and France, 19% in Germany — and many countries also have reduced rates for specific goods. Just type your rate into the rate field; the calculator accepts any percentage including decimals.

Does every sale include VAT?

No. Most VAT systems exempt or zero-rate certain categories — typically staple foods, medical care, education, financial services and exports — and small businesses under a turnover threshold may charge no VAT or a reduced simplified rate. This calculator assumes the standard rate applies; check your local rules for exempt items.

Are the amounts I enter sent to a server?

No. Every calculation runs entirely inside your browser and no network request is made with your figures. Invoice amounts, contract values and revenue numbers are safe to type in, and the page keeps working offline once it has loaded.
