      Which Format Should I Choose?

      Each format is good at a different job, and picking the right one is usually the whole point of converting in the first place.

      • WEBP → JPG for compatibility. More and more sites save images as WEBP, but some chat apps, older upload forms, and desktop programs still refuse to open them. Converting a WEBP to JPG makes it work everywhere — this is the single most common reason people convert an image, whether it is to attach it in a messenger, upload it to a government portal, or drop it into an old document editor.
      • Keep transparency as PNG. A company logo, an icon, or a signature cut-out needs a see-through background. If you convert those to JPG the transparent area turns solid white, which looks wrong on a colored page — so keep or convert them to PNG (or WEBP) to preserve the transparency.
      • Convert to WEBP to save space. For blogs, online stores, and websites, WEBP typically produces files 25–35% smaller than JPG at the same visual quality, which means faster page loads and better SEO. Every modern browser — Chrome, Edge, Safari, Samsung Internet — opens WEBP, so it is a safe choice for anything that lives online.
      • Use PNG for a lossless copy. Screenshots, diagrams, and images with sharp text or flat color stay crisp as PNG, with no compression artifacts around the edges. Converting a JPG to PNG will not recover detail that JPG already discarded, but it prevents any further loss if you plan to edit and re-save.
      💡 Tip: Converting to JPG or WEBP usually shrinks the file, but if you need to hit an exact size limit, run the result through the Image Compressor afterward. If you need specific pixel dimensions, resize first with the Image Resizer.

      A Note on HEIC (iPhone Photos)

      Photos taken on a recent iPhone are often saved as HEIC, Apple's high-efficiency format. Browsers cannot read HEIC, so no in-browser tool — including this one — can convert it directly. Rather than hand you a broken file, the converter detects HEIC uploads and clearly flags them as skipped. To use them here, set your iPhone to shoot JPG (Settings → Camera → Formats → "Most Compatible"), or open the photo in the Photos app and export or share it as a JPG first. Once it is a JPG, it converts here like any other image.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      Are my files really never uploaded to a server?

      Correct. The conversion happens entirely on your device using the canvas technology built into your browser. Open your browser's developer tools and check the Network tab — you won't see a single file upload request. Sensitive images like ID scans or family photos are safe to convert here.

      Which formats can I convert between?

      You can load JPG, PNG, WEBP, BMP, and static GIF files and convert them to JPG, PNG, or WEBP. That covers the everyday cases: WEBP to JPG for compatibility, PNG to WEBP to save space, or JPG to PNG when you need a lossless copy. Animated GIFs are flattened to their first frame.

      What happens to transparency when I convert PNG to JPG?

      JPG does not support transparency, so any transparent areas are filled with white when you convert a PNG or WEBP to JPG. If you need to keep a transparent background — for a logo or icon — choose PNG or WEBP as the output format instead. The tool tells you whenever transparent pixels were filled.

      Can I convert iPhone HEIC photos?

      Not directly. Browsers cannot read Apple's HEIC/HEIF format, so this tool detects HEIC files and skips them rather than producing a broken result. On your iPhone, go to Settings → Camera → Formats and choose 'Most Compatible' to shoot in JPG, or open the photo in the Photos app and share or export it as JPG first — then it converts here without any problem.

      Which format should I choose — JPG, PNG, or WEBP?

      Use JPG for photographs and anywhere you need maximum compatibility, such as chat apps and older upload forms. Use PNG when you need a transparent background or a lossless copy of graphics and screenshots. Use WEBP for the web when you want the smallest file at a given quality — it typically saves 25–35% versus JPG and supports transparency.
