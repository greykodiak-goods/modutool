      What Makes This Different from Other Compression Sites

      Most online compression services upload your photos to their own servers, process them there, and send them back. You have no way of knowing when the uploaded copies are deleted or who can see them along the way. For sensitive images — family photos, ID scans, contract documents — that should give you pause.

      ThisIsMyIMG's image compressor never sends your photos anywhere. The entire compression runs inside your computer (or phone) using the canvas technology built into your browser. That design brings three advantages.

      • Privacy — your photos never leave your device, so there is simply nothing to leak.
      • Speed — with no upload or download waits, even dozens of photos finish in seconds.
      • No limits — since we pay no server costs, there are no caps on file count, runs, or size.

      Which Settings Should I Use?

      Two things mostly determine an image's file size. One is compression quality — how precisely a lossy format like JPG or WEBP preserves color information. The other is resolution — the pixel count. Smartphone camera originals usually exceed 4000px, but for viewing on monitors and phone screens, 1920px on the longest side is plenty, so limiting resolution alone cuts file size dramatically.

      • Web posts and forum attachments — 70% quality + longest side 1920px. Nearly indistinguishable on screen, with the biggest savings.
      • Document submissions or possible printing — 80–90% quality + longest side 2560px, or keep the original resolution.
      • PNG screenshots and graphics — switching the output format to WEBP (recommended) or JPG shrinks them dramatically. Note that converting a transparent PNG to JPG fills the transparent areas with a background color, so pick WEBP if you need transparency.

      If a compressed result would come out larger than the original (an already-optimized image), the tool automatically keeps the original and tells you — so feel free to experiment as many times as you like. You can change the settings and recompress without re-adding your files.

      💡 Tip: Compression also helps protect your privacy — the process strips EXIF metadata, including GPS location and device details, so you can post photos to social media or marketplace listings without worrying about revealing where they were taken. If you need exact pixel dimensions instead, use the Image Resizer.

      Frequently Asked Questions

      Are my files really never uploaded to a server?

      Correct. Compression happens entirely on your device using the canvas technology built into your browser. Open your browser's developer tools and check the Network tab — you won't see a single file upload request. Sensitive images like ID scans or family photos are safe to use here.

      Will the quality loss be noticeable?

      At 80–90% quality, most photos show no difference visible to the naked eye. For web uploads or document attachments, 70–80% quality with the longest side limited to 1920px gives the best balance of file size and quality. Download the result, compare it, and if you're not happy, just change the settings and compress again.

      Which image formats are supported?

      JPG, PNG, WEBP, and BMP. iPhone HEIC files can't be read directly by browsers, so export them as JPG from your Photos app first. PNG is a lossless format, so compressing it as-is may not shrink it much — switching the output format to JPG or WEBP usually reduces it dramatically.

      I compressed an image but it didn't shrink — or it got bigger.

      If an image has already been heavily compressed, there may be no room left to shrink it, and the result can come out larger than the original. In that case the tool automatically keeps your original file and tells you. Limiting the maximum resolution to 1920px or converting to WEBP often reduces the size further.

      Does it work on phones? Are my original photos changed?

      Yes. It works in modern mobile browsers such as Chrome, Safari, and Samsung Internet. Your original files are never modified — the compressed versions are downloaded as new files.
