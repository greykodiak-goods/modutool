The four formulas

These are the exact formulas the calculator uses — handy for homework or for writing spreadsheet formulas:

  • B% of A — result = A × (B ÷ 100). Example: 50,000 × 0.3 = 15,000
  • A is what % of B — ratio (%) = (A ÷ B) × 100. Example: 154 ÷ 200 × 100 = 77%
  • Percent change — change (%) = ((B − A) ÷ A) × 100. The old value A is the base (denominator). Example: (3,050,000 − 2,800,000) ÷ 2,800,000 × 100 ≈ 8.93% increase
  • Discount — discount amount = original price × (rate ÷ 100); final price = original price − discount amount. Example: 30% off 59,000 → discount 17,700, final price 41,300

A common mistake — percent vs percentage points

This is the mix-up people make most. A percentage point (pp) is the simple difference between two percentage values, while percent (%) is change relative to a base. If an interest rate rises from 3% to 5%, the correct phrasing is "up 2 percentage points"; expressed as a growth rate it is (5 − 3) ÷ 3 × 100 ≈ 66.67%. Saying "rates went up 2%" would literally mean 3% → 3.06% — a completely different claim. Keeping % and pp apart is also the key to reading polling and market-share stories accurately.

One more: percentages don't stack by addition. 30% off followed by an extra 20% off is not 50% off but 0.7 × 0.8 = 0.56 — that is, 44% off. For the same reason, a stock that falls 50% must rise 100%, not 50%, to get back to even: the base (denominator) changes each time.

💡 Tip: For stacked discounts (e.g. 30% off plus an extra 20% coupon), use discount card ④ twice. First get the price after 30% off, then put that result into the original-price field and apply 20%. The order doesn't matter — 20% first, 30% second gives the same final price.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between percent (%) and percentage points (pp)?

A percentage point is the simple difference between two percentage values, while percent measures change relative to a base value. If an interest rate goes from 3% to 5%, that is a rise of 2 percentage points — but expressed as a growth rate it is (5 − 3) ÷ 3 × 100 ≈ 66.67%. This is one of the most commonly confused concepts in news stories about rates and polls.

Why can't percent change be calculated when the old value is 0?

The percent-change formula (B − A) ÷ A × 100 divides by the old value A, so when A is 0 it becomes division by zero, which is mathematically undefined. In that case the calculator shows 'Cannot calculate'. Double-check the base (old) value you entered.

How are decimal places handled?

Results are rounded to two decimal places for display, and shown without decimals when they come out to a whole number. Internal calculations use the exact unrounded values, so rounding errors never accumulate between steps.

Are the numbers I enter sent to or stored on a server?

No. Every calculation runs entirely inside the browser you are looking at, and no network request is made. Open the network tab in your browser's developer tools and you will see nothing transmitted; refreshing the page also clears the inputs. Sensitive figures like salaries or revenue are safe to enter.

How do I calculate stacked discounts (e.g. 30% off plus an extra 20%)?

Stacked discounts apply in sequence, not by adding up. 30% + 20% is not 50% off but 0.7 × 0.8 = 0.56, i.e. 44% off. In the discount card, first get the final price after 30% off, then enter that result as the original price and apply 20% to get the exact final price.
