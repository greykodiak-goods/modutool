    Why Stamp a Watermark on Your Photos

    A visible watermark is the simplest way to say "this image is mine." Whether you run a shopping mall, post product shots to a marketplace, share work on Instagram, or publish photos on a blog, a text stamp makes ownership obvious the moment someone screenshots or re-uploads your picture. It won't stop every thief, but it turns silent copying into an obvious, attributable act — and for most casual reposting, that deterrent alone is enough.

    ThisIsMyPDF's watermark tool never sends your files anywhere. The text is painted onto your images inside your own browser using canvas technology, and nothing is uploaded to a server. That matters when the photos are unpublished designs, client work under embargo, or personal pictures you'd rather not hand to an unknown website. It also means there are no limits: stamp a hundred product photos in one batch without a per-file cap, a queue, or a sign-up wall.

    Three watermark layouts cover the common needs. Bottom-right keeps a small, tasteful mark out of the way of the subject — ideal for a © line or handle you want present but not distracting. Center drops one larger mark over the middle of the frame, useful for proof photos or drafts you're sharing for review. Tiled repeats the text diagonally across the entire image at a 45° angle, so there is no clean corner left to crop away — the strongest option when theft protection is the whole point.

    Because the watermark is drawn with system fonts rather than an embedded font file, you can type Korean, Japanese, accented characters, or emoji and they render correctly — something a PDF stamp usually can't do without extra font handling. The size is expressed as a percentage of each image's width, so a single setting looks proportional whether the photo is 800px or 6,000px wide. A subtle shadow keeps white text legible over bright skies and dark text legible over shadows, and the tool automatically applies the EXIF orientation of smartphone photos so nothing comes out sideways. Every file keeps its original format — JPG stays JPG, PNG and WEBP keep their transparency — and outputs are renamed _watermarked so your originals are safe.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Are my photos really never uploaded to a server?

    Correct. The watermark is drawn onto your images entirely on your device using the canvas feature built into your browser. Open your browser's developer tools and check the Network tab — you won't see a single file upload request. Product shots, personal photos, and unpublished work are all safe to stamp here.

    Can I use Korean, emoji, or other non-English text as the watermark?

    Yes. Unlike a PDF tool that has to embed a font file, this watermark is painted with your device's own system fonts, so Korean, Japanese, accented Latin characters, and most emoji render correctly. The exact letterforms depend on the fonts installed on the machine that runs the tool, not on the viewer's device, because the text is baked into the pixels of the image.

    Which watermark position should I choose?

    Bottom-right is the classic choice for a discreet copyright or handle that doesn't cover the subject. Center places a single large mark over the middle of the photo, which is harder to crop out. Tiled repeats the text diagonally across the whole image at a 45° angle — the safest option against theft, since there is no clean area left to reuse.

    Does the tool keep my original file format?

    Yes. A JPG stays a JPG, a PNG stays a PNG, and a WEBP stays a WEBP, so transparency in PNG and WEBP files is preserved. GIF and BMP files are flattened and saved as PNG because those formats can't be re-encoded from the browser canvas. Every download is named originalname_watermarked so your originals are never overwritten.

    Can someone still remove the watermark I add?

    A watermark is baked into the image pixels, so it can't be toggled off — but a determined editor can crop or paint over a small corner mark. For strong protection use the tiled pattern with medium opacity and the readability shadow on, which leaves no untouched region to reconstruct. A visible watermark deters casual reposting and makes ownership obvious even when it can't make theft impossible.
